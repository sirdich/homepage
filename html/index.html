What is Linux?

Linux is a family of open-source Unix-like operating systems based on the Linux kernel, an operating system kernel first released on September 17, 1991, by Linus Torvalds. Because Linux is known as a Free and Open Source Software (FOSS), it is possible for anyone to read and modify the source code. It also means that it's complete free to use and distribute, giving you back your computer, free from spying eyes and forced obsilescence. Because of this, there are hundreds of different distributions, or "distros" to choose from. 3 of those distros are considered the "big three" and are the basis for many other distros. Those distros are Debian, Arch and Redhat.

You can of course choose one of the big three on it's own, and still have the freedom to add all the bells and whistles of other distributions, but that comes at the cost of a relatively steep learning curve and your time. This website will give you a brief overview of the big three, and highlight some of the more popular distros based on them.
